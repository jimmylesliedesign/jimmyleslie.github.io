Product Finder

Bunnings Warehouse

Bunnings customers’ number one problem was finding products in-store. Using design sprints, great research, and a little bit of luck, we solved that problem – and created a multi-million dollar revenue channel along the way.

Released

Spring 2021

Role

Design lead

Summary

Problem

Customers’ number one problem is finding products in the warehouse-format stores.

Team members spend most of their time telling customers where products are.

Brief

How can we help customers find what they’re looking for without having to ask team members?

Approach

I led a small product team through rapid product design sprints, plus a trial with 700+ customers.

Outcome

The app hit #1 on the Australian app store for shopping apps.

It also formed the basis of a new multi-million dollar mobile ecommerce channel for Bunnings.

•

Understanding the problem

Previous research identified that finding products in-store was customers’ number one problem. A subsequent Kano study also revealed product finding as Bunnings’ top opportunity for CSAT.

When surveyed, Bunnings Warehouse team members said that 75% of their time on the shop floor was spent directing customers to product locations. This means that for most of the day, staff aren’t able to help customers with their in-depth product knowledge, or carry out essential daily work tasks.

Creating a proof of concept

An early proof of concept was created using a Messenger bot, which we usability tested in-store. Customers did not find a chatbot solution accessible enough, as they relied heavily on product imagery and the layout of the store when trying to find products.

Customer expectations of a native app

A Google trends study suggested that customers already expected there to be a Bunnings app in-market. In combination with findings of the the chatbot testing, this suggested that a native app might be a better format to help solve the problem.

Kicking off a series of design sprints

We gathered a diverse group of stakeholders who would evolve into the core product team. I led several sprints to align on the problem, and to gather ideas that would inform our early concepts.

Lightning demos and crazy 8s style exercises resulted in a few different approaches to trying to solve our goal. Some of our early ideas included image search to improve product identification, organised shopping lists to streamline store visits, and an interactive store map, to help with wayfinding.

Iterating and refining

After aligning on an MVP, we proceeded to build and test with customers in-store. A notable change made post-testing was scaling up imagery and pricing on the product cards, as we noticed that customers would primarily use these as reference points for finding the products on the shelves.

“This solves the biggest problem I have at Bunnings. It can be very frustrating not just finding what you’re looking for, but finding people to ask.”

Test participant

Although all products are all mapped to an aisle and bay location in the backend, in-store signage does not include bay number. We developed an interactive store map from store CAD drawings to visualise bay location in the aisle, guiding customers to exact product locations.

Testing at scale

To gain more quantitative insight, we set up a beta trial with over 700 test participants. We recruited participants by directly emailing frequent Bunnings customers with an invitation to sign up to the trial. Participants could leave feedback whenever they wanted via an omnipresent feedback button in the app.

An unexpected release strategy

The beta trial was a huge success, and gave us a projected app rating, indication of retention rate and qualitative insights to use moving forward.

The COVID-19 pandemic expedited the release of the app as a means of reducing time spent in-store. The app shot to the top of the app store, and received rave reviews from press and customers.

Foundations for scale

Almost immediately after releasing the Product Finder, we began working on an ecommerce strategy for the app. I left the project in 2021 after completing most of the ecommerce design. In 2023, the app was updated with full online shopping capability, opening up a multi-million dollar revenue channel for Bunnings.
