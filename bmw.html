Boosting test drive conversion in two weeks

BMW

A quick, lean redesign of BMW's test drive booking experience.

Released

2020

Role

Design lead

Summary

Problem

A larger than expected number of users were dropping off during the test drive booking flow.

Approach

I was onboarded onto this project late in the game due to some early struggles with UX resourcing which meant we had to start from scratch. I led the design of the project and worked alongside another visual designer moving into handover.

Approach

Within two weeks, I redesigned, tested and delivered final designs that demonstrated a significant improvement in testing.

Outcome

16% increase in conversion rate for test drive bookings.

•

Understanding the problem

In order to understand the problems behind the previous design (below), I organised exploratory interviews with people who were considering purchasing a new car.

Participants demonstrated an overall dislike towards the front-on view of the cars, as BMW's distinctive grill and headlights make it difficult to distinguish between models. Participants also showed difficulty finding certain models due to unclear groupings.

Improving discoverability

The exploratory interviews also revealed different browsing behaviours, including a want to search by body type rather than series. To combat this, I opted for a more angular view of the cars to showcase more of their shapes, and also included a body type filter.

I also chose to display all of the models upfront without the need to click-and-discover, in an attempt to solve the grouping confusion (re-organising model grouping was out of scope).

Testing

Within a week, I created a prototype and moderation guide, and recruited 6 participants to test the new design with. The test focused on model discoverability, and the time taken to complete the booking process using both the proposed update and the old design. I also conducted an unmoderated preference test for additional quantitative insight.

Results

The time taken to complete the booking process was significantly reduced using the updated design. Participants found the newer design easier to scan and understand, and the time taken to find and select a specific model was also significantly reduced. Additionally, the preference test showed an 87% lean towards the updated design.

Finalising the design

Following testing, I worked alongside one other visual designer to finalise the designs and prepare them for handover. After uplifting the designs with BMW branding, we created a series of Figma prototypes to illustrate flows and motion principles in order to make handover as seamless as possible.

Kicking off a series of design sprints

We gathered a diverse group of stakeholders who would evolve into the core product team. I led several sprints to align on the problem, and to gather ideas that would inform our early concepts.

Outcome

This project was a great exercise in lean UX. Our time constraints meant that we had to be strategic in understanding which corners we could afford to cut without compromising the quality of the design.

Taking time to fully understand the problem helped me to be confident that I was tackling the right issues before I started, and leveraging methods like unmoderated testing provided quick and easy quantitative support to our qualitative findings.
