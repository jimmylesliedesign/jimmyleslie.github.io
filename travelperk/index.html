Managing conflicting stakeholder visions

TravelPerk / Delta Airlines

Overview

I led a critical redesign project that rescued a crucial partnership, improved our user experience, and created a unified design language across our booking platform. In the process, I also uncovered a significant revenue opportunity that substantially increased our flight booking revenue.

Problem

Delta Airlines' B2C-focused design requirements conflicted with TravelPerk's B2B user needs. The project also faced technical constraints, misaligned stakeholder expectations, and a rigid deadline.

My role

As Product Design Lead and de facto project manager, I was responsible for establishing clear processes, aligning stakeholders, and balancing Delta's requirements with user needs.

Approach

I led all design work from concept to implementation, created a scope document with clear success criteria, conducted a heuristic review to identify improvement opportunities, and discovered an opportunity to expand revenue by extending flexible fare offerings.

Outcome

We secured the Delta partnership while improving key metrics, fixed legacy UX issues, established a unified design system, and increased revenue through expanded cancellation coverage availability.

The challenge

A B2B world

TravelPerk is a B2B tool that makes it easy for people to book and manage company travel. Flights are the most-commonly-booked type of travel.

A B2C requirement

Delta Airlines was enforcing new guidelines for how vendors should display their products. The guidelines were created for leisure travel, as a way to promote and upsell more profitable flight fares.

The conflict

• Employers actively instruct employees using TravelPerk to avoid upselling attempts, in order to maintain strict policy budgets

• This creates a fundamental misalignment between Delta's goals and TravelPerk users' goals

• Not adhering to the guidelines meant risking losing Delta's inventory, which would have been detrimental to TravelPerk's US market presence

Approach

1. Creating guidelines

Defining success

  • I led the creation of a comprehensive definition of done document that was agreed on by both TravelPerk and Delta stakeholders
  • This became our guiding reference, helping us maintain focus, prevent scope creep, and ground contentious conversations

Encouraging feedback

  • Fortnightly critiques with designers from other product verticals to maintain alignment
  • Weekly reviews with engineering to prevent surprises
  • Regular usability testing for frequent design evaluation

Balancing communication

  • Avoiding technical language where possible when working with Delta stakeholders
  • Moving contentious feedback offline to evaluate against our agreed scope, avoiding unproductive debates while preserving collaboration

2. Starting with research

• I surveyed 1,000 customers about their flight booking behaviour

• The research conclusively showed flight time as customers' top priority when booking flights

• Delta's guidelines proposed hiding return flight time options until the user selected an outbound flight

• TravelPerk users can only book in-policy journeys, so visibility of outbound and return combinations that are in-policy is critical

3. Documenting existing problems

The redesign provided a great opportunity to address legacy usability issues. Through a heuristic evaluation with designers from all booking verticals, we identified several improvement opportunities.

4. Evaluating at each turn

We usability tested all changes and implemented a phased rollout to monitor metrics. Observing real user sessions throughout the rollout also revealed great actionable insights. We also implemented a CES widget to quantify and monitor usability.

The solution

Breaking down the new design

Focusing on the details

Our heuristic evaluation revealed many overlooked areas for improvement. Redesigning the entire flow gave us the ability to apply a more-considered approach to every visual element.

Strategic wins

Despite Delta's rigid guidelines, I negotiated some key adjustments: keeping the outbound and return flight overview, and reducing fare categories from 6 to 4. These adaptations prioritise customer needs and reduce upselling intensity, while meeting Delta's core requirements.

Before
After

Searching and filtering

In the old design, search parameters were hidden behind a click-to-reveal interaction, despite how frequently users would adjust terms when exploring flight options.

In the new design, search parameters are front-and-centre. They move out of view when a user scrolls down the page, but re-appear wherever they scroll up, making it easy to adjust them at any time. We also added a date nudge button to make it easier to explore flights on different days.

Updating the filtering experience from a side panel to a horizontal progressive disclosure pattern accommodated Delta's requirement for more horizontal space, while fixing IA and visual hierarchy issues that had plagued the previous design.

A localised experience

After discovering that the new design resulted in a many empty results for European searches, we created separate region-specific designs: North American results follow Delta's guidelines, while other markets see a simpler design to accommodate fewer product variations.

Fixing consistency across verticals

We prioritised standardising the booking experience across all verticals as a core redesign goal. Each pattern was designed for cross-vertical compatibility and evaluated by all teams throughout the process.

Bonus

A hidden revenue opportunity

In the previous design, all users would see the FlexiFare option as part of the booking flow. In the new single-screen flow, only users that selected the "Basic" category would see a FlexiFare, risking a reduction in visibility.

Increasing visibility

I proposed expanding FlexiFare to the Basic and Standard categories, which would boost visibility and increase revenue potential due to the percentage-based pricing.

The project succeeded on multiple fronts, delivering both business and user experience improvements. The new 'standard' Flexifare variant increased attachment, representing a huge revenue increase. Learnings

Establish shared goals early

The definition of done document became our most-valuable tool for alignment and decision-making. By securing upfront agreement on scope and success criteria, we could resolve conflicts efficiently and maintain momentum.

Observation beyond release

Post-launch user behaviour observation revealed improvement opportunities invisible in pre-release testing. Real-world interactions provided insights that testing and quantitative data alone couldn't capture.

Find opportunity in constraints

What began as a challenging and disruptive project ultimately became a platform for fixing cross-vertical design issues and opening new revenue streams. The constraints forced creative thinking, proving that unexpected challenges can often lead to innovative change.
